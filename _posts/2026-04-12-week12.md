I previously thought open source projects oppose with business. In reality, many companies rely heavily on free and open-source software, and many also participate in producing it. 

Companies often do not make money by selling software in the traditional sense. Instead, they build business models around things such as support, hosting. Even if the code itself is open, companies can still profit from the services and infrastructure built around it.

I recently saw an example of this while looking into AppFlowy, an open-source alternative to Notion. I came across a person who seemed to be using the product in a company or professional setting. They had opened many issues in the repository and even recorded a 30-minute video demonstrating bugs they had found and features they wanted to request. What stood out to me was that the maintainer team responded quite promptly and thanked them for bringing these issues up, since this kind of feedback helps improve the product and supports its development.

It showed that industry participation in open source is not limited to contributing code. When a company or professional user opening issues, documenting bugs, requesting features, In a way, they are helping shape the direction of the project based on real-world business needs.
